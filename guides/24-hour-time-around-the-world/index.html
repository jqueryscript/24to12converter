24-Hour Time Around the World

Updated June 20, 2026

Most of the world uses the 24-hour clock for written schedules, timetables, and official communication. The main exception is the English-speaking world and former British colonies. If you are traveling, the simplest rule is: if the country is in Europe or Latin America, expect 24-hour. If it is a former British colony, expect 12-hour. Here is the full breakdown.

Direct answer

In Europe, Latin America, and much of Asia, written times use 24-hour format: 18:30 means 6:30 PM. In the US, Canada, Australia, India, the Philippines, and most former British colonies, written times use 12-hour with AM/PM. In conversation, nearly everyone converts to 12-hour regardless of what is written on the schedule. Use the converter to check any time.

The British Empire pattern

There is a simple way to predict which format a country uses: check whether it was part of the British Empire. Countries that were British colonies or dominions — the US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, India, Pakistan, South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, the Philippines — overwhelmingly use 12-hour time for daily life.

Countries that were not in the British sphere — continental Europe, Latin America, East Asia — standardized on 24-hour time in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

The exception that proves the rule: Francophone Africa. Senegal, Cote d'Ivoire, Mali, and the Democratic Republic of Congo all use the 24-hour clock in writing, following the French pattern rather than the British one. Their neighbors across the border in Anglophone Africa use 12-hour.

This split is also about infrastructure timing. European countries built national railway networks in the late 1800s and early 1900s. Cross-border trains — Paris to Berlin, Milan to Vienna — needed unambiguous timetables. The 24-hour clock solved that. Britain and the US built railways earlier, when the 12-hour convention was still unchallenged, and never retrofitted their systems.

Country spotlights

France

Strict 24-hour in writing. Uses "h" instead of colon: 14h30. Spoken: "deux heures et demie" (two thirty), never "quatorze heures trente."

Germany

24-hour everywhere, even in casual text messages. Spoken German often uses 24-hour: "vierzehn Uhr" is normal for 2 PM. One of the few countries where 24h made it into speech.

Brazil

Written: 24-hour. Spoken: 12-hour with "da manha" (morning), "da tarde" (afternoon), "da noite" (night). A store sign says "8h-18h" but people say "oito da manha as seis da tarde."

Japan

Trains, TV listings, and official documents: 24-hour. Conversation: 12-hour with "gozen" (AM) and "gogo" (PM). A uniquely clean split between written and spoken.

Canada

A country divided. English Canada uses 12-hour. Quebec uses 24-hour in writing, following French convention. Government documents are bilingual in both formats.

United Kingdom

The most visible mixed-format country. BBC and National Rail use 24-hour. Daily conversation and informal writing: 12-hour. A Brit reads "19:00" on a train ticket and says "seven o'clock."

For travelers: how to read times abroad

If you are traveling from a 12-hour country to a 24-hour country, you will see 24-hour times everywhere:

  • Train and bus tickets. The departure and arrival times are always 24-hour. 18:30 means early evening, not morning.
  • Flight confirmations. Non-US airlines use 24-hour on boarding passes and confirmations.
  • Hotel check-in signs. "Check-in: 14:00. Check-out: 11:00." You will see this exact format across Europe.
  • Museum and attraction hours. "Open 10:00-18:00" means 10 AM to 6 PM.
  • Restaurant reservations. Booking confirmations from European restaurants use 24-hour. "19:30" is 7:30 PM.
  • Concert and event tickets. Doors at 19:00, show at 20:00.

Quick mental conversion: If the hour is above 12, subtract 12. 14:00 = 2 PM. 19:30 = 7:30 PM. 22:00 = 10 PM. After a day or two of seeing these, the conversion becomes automatic. For a full chart, see the time conversion chart.
